# Space — March 2026

## 1. [NASA rolls Artemis II SLS and Orion to Launch Pad 39B](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Artemis_II)
**Source:** Wikipedia (Artemis II) · **Date:** 2026-03-20

Why this matters: NASA rolled the SLS rocket and Orion spacecraft to Launch Pad 39B (after a wind-related first attempt on March 18), staging the first crewed lunar mission in 50+ years for its April launch. Rollout is the irreversible commitment step — Artemis II is now real, not slipping, and the lunar-economy investment thesis hinges on whether it succeeds.

## 2. [SpaceX launches 29 Starlink satellites on Falcon 9](https://spaceflightnow.com/2026/03/18/live-coverage-spacex-to-launch-29-starlink-satellites-on-falcon-9-rocket-from-cape-canaveral-11/)
**Source:** Spaceflight Now · **Date:** 2026-03-19

Why this matters: A routine 29-satellite Starlink mission, but routine is the point — SpaceX has industrialized launch cadence to where individual missions are background infrastructure. Starlink constellation density continues to grow, with downstream implications for orbital traffic management and global broadband economics.

## 3. [ESA launches first two Celeste (LEO-PNT) satellites on Rocket Lab Electron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_spaceflight_launches_in_January%E2%80%93March_2026)
**Source:** Wikipedia (Spaceflight launches Q1 2026) · **Date:** 2026-03-28

Why this matters: ESA's LEO-based positioning-navigation-timing constellation begins deployment — the European answer to GPS resilience concerns. Launched on a US small-launcher (Rocket Lab), it's also a quiet sign that European space sovereignty doesn't yet extend to small-sat launch capacity.

## 4. [China's Sustain Space demonstrates flexible robotic arm for on-orbit servicing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_spaceflight_launches_in_January%E2%80%93March_2026)
**Source:** Wikipedia (Spaceflight launches Q1 2026) · **Date:** 2026-03-16

Why this matters: Chinese commercial firm Sustain Space ran on-orbit ops with a flexible robotic arm on its Xiyuan-0 satellite — for satellite servicing and refueling. On-orbit servicing is the next major commercial frontier (extending satellite lifetimes vs. replacing them), and China is now a credible commercial player there, not just an observer.
